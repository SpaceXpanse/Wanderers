Newton's law of gravitation

Half a century after Johannes Kepler discovered the laws according to which planets move with respect to the sun, Sir Isaac Newton (1642-1727) formulated his law of gravitation, which is fundamental to the understanding of the dynamics of orbital motion, and from which Kepler's laws can be directly derived.

The law of gravitation states that an attracting force exists between two bodies, which is proportional to their inverse square distance. The magnitude of the force also depends on a physical property of the two bodies, called their mass.

To understand this, note that the numerator, r12, is the distance vector between body 1 and 2, that is, a vector with its origin at the position of body 1, and its tip at the position of body 2. The denominator, |r12|, (the vector enclosed in vertical bars) means the norm or length of the vector. Dividing a vector by its length always results in a vector of length 1, pointing in the same direction as the original vector.

So in a nutshell:
  • Two bodies of mass m1 and m2 at distance r will experience an attracting force of equal magnitude but opposite direction.
  • The magnitude of the force is proportional to the product of the masses of the two bodies, and inversely proportional to the square of their distance.
  • The proportionality constant is given by the gravitational constant G.
